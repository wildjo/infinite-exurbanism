Why the technologies of abundance that built the metropolis of today will lead us back to where it all began…

The Rural to Urban Pipeline

In 1950 barely 30% of humanity lived in cities; by 2020 that share exceeded 56%, powered by highways, antibiotics, silicon chips, and the promise of steady wages. Urbanists framed density as civilization’s best climate hack — shorter commutes, smaller homes, more intentional communities. (Glaeser et al) That narrative still holds truth, yet the tools that brought about this change are still at work, eliminating commutes, changing the definition of home, and shifting our definitions of meaning and intentionality. High-bandwidth networks, solar micro-grids, and autonomous logistics are erasing the old requirement to pile people and capital on the same GPS coordinates.

This presentation maps two crossing curves. One is the social trend of urbanization that has dominated since the Industrial Revolution. The other is a suite of technological trends — remote work, automation, cheap clean energy — whose long arcs now bend opportunity outward, toward smaller towns, “rurban” fringes, and regenerated village cores. Follow the inflection and you glimpse an ecological, economical future that feels less like sprawl and more like Infinite Exurbanism.

Numerous Factors Drive Migration

Jobs first. Factory floors and office towers concentrated capital and talent. A single percentage-point rise in urban population correlated with roughly 1% higher GDP per capita through the post-war boom (United Nations). Cities also promised cultural ferment: jazz clubs, universities, queer bars, and immigrant kitchens, all within a subway ride.

Community can bring people together but it can also drive people apart. While the benefits of community are many, overcrowding and civil unrest can drive residents to yearn for what they lack. Migration is a fact of life.

Tech that eased the crowd. Elevators, concrete, and HVAC stacked people skyward; penicillin tamed density’s diseases; later, broadband turned any cafe into a trading floor. Suburbs emerged as a middle-class compromise—commutable distance paired with a patch of lawn—but they still orbited the metro core.

Innovation in itself isn’t a panacea to individual and societal ills. Yet, innovations in the distribution of goods and services allow for concentration of population in urban centers that have brought tremendous value to the human cultural experience. But, when further innovation drives down costs of distribution and alters the tradeoff, will migratory pressures of rural scarcity for urban plenty still drive behavior?

We dreamed of greener pastures, while making home where we could: building canyons of glass and steel, plowing fields of concrete and paint…

Liberating Community from Location

Until recently, it has been easier for people to move where the resources were. Migration patterns for all species follow similar trends: towards resources and away from dangers. Technological advancements are changing that equation, allowing us to better manage resources and enhance safety. Three accelerating trends flatten the old locational pyramid:

  1. Remote work. U.S. full-time remote employment leapt from 4% pre-2020 to 26% and remains above 20%, extending professional job markets far beyond city limits. (Parker)
  2. Automation. Robotics and AI carve the labor share out of manufacturing, logistics, and even surgery, shrinking the wage premium of big ports and rail hubs.
  3. Cheap clean kilowatts. Solar-PV costs fell 89% between 2010-2023; rural micro-grids now power farms and workshops miles from a substation. (International Energy Agency)

The same fiber backbones that once funneled data into skyscrapers now let architects sketch from Montana and radiologists read scans from Oaxaca. When productivity floats in the cloud, the gravitational pull of the business district weakens.

Toward Infinite Exurbanism

Combine those vectors and a new settlement pattern emerges:

  • Micro-communities of 500 to 5,000 people, stitched by fiber and drone, share maker-spaces, community solar, and bioremediation wetlands.
  • Polycentric regions replace the spoke-and-hub commute: autonomous e-shuttles link villages for weekly markets; most daily interaction happens online.
  • Ecological dividends flow not from an urban exodus but from load-balancing. Less land is paved for parking; distributed agriculture shortens supply chains; secondary forests reclaim vacated lots.
The shift is incremental—families leaving the Bay Area for Boise, freelancers trading Brooklyn rent for Catskills homesteads—but the curve is bending. The UN projects that by 2035, absolute growth in small-town populations will outpace that of mega-cities for the first time. Place is becoming a choice again, and that transforms citizenship from passive residence to active design challenge.

Your Turn as Architect

If home and work are decoupled, communities can compete on meaning, not just wages. Rural cooperatives that embrace broadband, universal design, and circular economics will attract talent and capital—without importing sprawl. The next decade’s civic innovators will write zoning that welcomes accessory dwellings, spectrum policy that treats wireless like water, and cultural storytelling that paints the countryside as lab, not museum. In short: we are called, in Buckminster Fuller’s words, “to be architects of the future, not its victims.”

Below are two levers of technological progress—energy production that is local, renewable, and environmentally neutral; and communications infrastructure that replaces the need to move goods and people physically, enabling mass-scale, distributed problem-solving. Alongside these is a social lever: progress toward work autonomy, where individuals contribute directly to their own welfare, their immediate social group, and the broader community—freely chosen, not imposed from above, and benefiting all involved.

Innovation in these three areas alone is enough to bend the curve back toward ruralization, and yet these are just three of a myriad of factors that are influencing ongoing change. The humans drive to create meaning and purpose remains the most powerful factor of all. What future for yourself and your community will you be building towards?
