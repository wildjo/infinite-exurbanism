Why the technologies of abundance
that built the metropolis of today
will lead us back to where it all began…

1. A Century on Fast-Forward

In 1950 barely 30% of humanity lived in cities; by 2020 that share exceeded 56%, powered by highways, antibiotics, silicon chips, and the promise of steady wages. Urbanists framed density as civilization’s best climate hack—shorter commutes, smaller homes, richer streets. That narrative still holds truth, yet the tools it celebrated are quietly rewiring the equation. High-bandwidth networks, solar micro-grids, and autonomous logistics are erasing the old requirement to pile people and capital on the same GPS coordinates.

This presentation maps two crossing curves. One is the social trend of urbanization that has dominated since the Industrial Revolution. The other is a suite of technological trends—remote work, automation, cheap clean energy—whose long arcs now bend opportunity outward, toward smaller towns, “rurban” fringes, and regenerated village cores. Follow the inflection and you glimpse an ecological, economical future that feels less like sprawl and more like Infinite Exurbanism.

2. Why We Packed Into Cities

Jobs first. Factory floors and office towers concentrated capital and talent. A single percentage-point rise in urban population correlated with roughly 1 % higher GDP per capita through the post-war boom.1 Cities also promised cultural ferment: jazz clubs, universities, queer bars, and immigrant kitchens, all within a subway ride.

Tech that eased the crowd. Elevators, concrete, and HVAC stacked people skyward; penicillin tamed density’s diseases; later, broadband turned any cafe into a trading floor. Suburbs emerged as a middle-class compromise—commutable distance paired with a patch of lawn—but they still orbited the metro core.

3. The Quiet Pivot — Technology Unchains Place

Three accelerating trends flatten the old locational pyramid:

  1. Remote work. U.S. full-time remote employment leapt from 4 % pre-2020 to 26 % and remains above 20 %, extending professional job markets far beyond city limits.3
  2. Automation. Robotics and AI carve the labor share out of manufacturing, logistics, and even surgery, shrinking the wage premium of big ports and rail hubs.
  3. Cheap clean kilowatts. Solar-PV costs fell 89 % between 2010-2023; rural micro-grids now power farms and workshops miles from a substation.4

The same fiber backbones that once funneled data into skyscrapers now let architects sketch from Montana and radiologists read scans from Oaxaca. When productivity floats in the cloud, the gravitational pull of the CBD weakens.

4. Toward Infinite Exurbanism

Combine those vectors and a new settlement pattern emerges:

  • Micro-communities of 500 – 5 000 people, stitched by fiber and drone, share maker-spaces, community solar, and bioremediation wetlands.
  • Polycentric regions replace the spoke-and-hub commute: autonomous e-shuttles link villages for weekly markets; most daily interaction happens online.
  • Ecological dividends flow not from an urban exodus but from load-balancing. Less land is paved for parking; distributed agriculture shortens supply chains; secondary forests reclaim vacated lots.
The shift is incremental—families leaving the Bay Area for Boise, freelancers trading Brooklyn rent for Catskills homesteads—but the curve is bending. The UN projects that by 2035, absolute growth in small-town populations will outpace that of mega-cities for the first time.1 Place is becoming a choice again, and that transforms citizenship from passive residence to active design challenge.

5. Your Turn as Architect

If home and work are decoupled, communities can compete on meaning, not just wages. Rural cooperatives that embrace broadband, universal design, and circular economics will attract talent and capital—without importing sprawl. The next decade’s civic innovators will write zoning that welcomes accessory dwellings, spectrum policy that treats wireless like water, and cultural storytelling that paints the countryside as lab, not museum. In short: we are called, in Buckminster Fuller’s words, “to be architects of the future, not its victims.” The drawings start now—where will you pin your next village on the map?
